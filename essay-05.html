After the Break

Discontinuity, lost trajectories, and the limits of reconstruction

There are moments that do not look important when they happen.

No visible rupture. No immediate consequence. Nothing that forces attention.

The system continues.

From the outside, nothing has changed.

From the inside, something has shifted.

---

Continuity as assumption

When we observe a system over time, we assume continuity.

We believe that between two observations, the system has evolved smoothly.

This assumption is rarely questioned.

It is convenient. It allows reconstruction.

It allows explanation.

But it is not guaranteed.

---

Hidden discontinuity

A system may undergo a change that is not captured.

No observation coincides with it.

No signal is recorded.

The next observation belongs to a different regime.

The observer connects the points.

A curve is drawn between them.

The discontinuity disappears.

---

Irreversibility

Some changes are not reversible.

In the sense that the previous trajectory cannot be recovered.

The system has entered a different region.

The structure has been modified.

Even if behavior returns to a previous form, the path is not the same.

---

Loss of information

When a transition is not observed, information is lost.

This loss is structural.

The system can no longer be reconstructed with certainty.

Multiple trajectories become compatible with the same observations.

The observer cannot decide.

Both paths are consistent.

The difference lies in what was not seen.

---

Delayed consequences

The effect of a discontinuity is rarely immediate.

The system may continue to produce acceptable outputs.

Stability may persist for a time.

The difference appears later.

When the system is required to rely on a structure that no longer exists.

---

False repair

There is an attempt to reconstruct the past.

To return to a previous state.

To reintroduce conditions that once produced stability.

This does not restore the trajectory.

It produces a new configuration that resembles the old one.

The difference is subtle.

It appears under constraint.

---

The cost of gaps

Not all observation gaps have the same effect.

Some occur in regions where the system is stable.

Others occur at points where the system is sensitive.

The impact depends on the local structure.

A small gap at the wrong moment has a larger effect than a long gap elsewhere.

---

What remains

Continuity is often assumed.

It should not be.

A system that is not observed continuously cannot be reconstructed completely.

Some transitions will be lost.

Some paths will never be known.

This does not prevent understanding, but it limits certainty.

And it changes the nature of what it means to follow a system over time.
