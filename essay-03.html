When Attention Splits

Multi-agent perception and the instability of the shared field

A child alone is already a complex system.

A child in the presence of another is not the same system.

Something changes before any word is spoken.

The presence of another introduces a second field. Not only an external input, but a structure that must be taken into account, anticipated, interpreted.

The system expands.

---

From one to two

When a child is alone, the system can be described by a single evolving state.

When another person appears, the system is no longer defined by one trajectory.

It becomes a coupled configuration.

The child is no longer reacting only to events, but to another system that is itself reacting.

This interaction is not linear.

The child observes the other, plus how the other might observe them.

A second layer appears.

---

The multiplication of perspectives

With two agents, the system already contains multiple perspectives.

With three, the situation changes again.

The child is no longer dealing with individual relations. The child begins to consider relations between others.

Who agrees with whom. Who is aligned. Who is watching whom.

The system becomes combinatorial.

The number of possible interpretations increases.

So does the uncertainty.

---

Attention as a limited resource

The child does not have unlimited capacity to process this complexity.

Attention must be distributed.

It can be focused, or it can be split.

When it is split, precision decreases.

Some signals are amplified. Others disappear.

A child who speaks clearly in a one-to-one interaction may hesitate in a group.

The state has not disappeared.

The conditions for its expression have changed.

---

The role of familiarity

Not all observers are equal.

Some are familiar. Their reactions are known, predictable.

Others are uncertain. Their reactions must be inferred in real time.

The system does not allocate attention equally.

It gives priority to uncertainty.

This changes the internal balance.

---

Misinterpretation

From the outside, changes in behavior are often interpreted as inconsistency.

A child who is calm in one context and unstable in another is said to have changed.

What has changed is the configuration of the system.

The internal state interacts differently with different structures.

---

Collapse of the field

There are situations where the system can no longer maintain coherence across all perspectives.

Too many observers. Too many possible interpretations.

The system simplifies.

It reduces its behavior to a minimal pattern.

Silence. Withdrawal. Repetition.

This is a response to a configuration that exceeds the system’s current capacity.

---

What remains

Human systems evolve in fields formed by others.

Understanding a child requires more than observing the child alone.

It requires understanding the structure of the field in which the child is placed.

Who is present. Who is absent. Who is predictable. Who is not.

What is stable. What is uncertain.

The same internal state does not produce the same behavior in every field.

What appears as inconsistency is often the trace of a deeper interaction.
