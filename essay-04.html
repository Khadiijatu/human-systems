Attention Has a Threshold

Signal, saturation, and the loss of resolution

Attention is often treated as a quantity.

More attention is considered better than less. Lack of attention is described as a deficit. Presence of attention is described as care.

This way of speaking hides something essential.

Attention has structure.

It has limits.

It has regimes.

Attention as a signal

When an adult observes a child, something is transmitted.

Not only words. Not only instructions. A signal.

This signal carries information about what is noticed, what is ignored, what is considered relevant.

The child receives this signal and adjusts.

Some parts of the internal state are reinforced. Others are weakened. Some are pushed to the surface. Others remain latent.

Attention shapes the projection.

Below the threshold

There is a level below which attention does not produce a stable effect.

Signals exist, but they are too weak, too irregular, too dispersed.

The system receives fragments that cannot be integrated.

In this regime, the child experiments.

Outputs vary. Patterns are tested. Reactions are probed.

From the outside, this appears as instability.

From the inside, it is a search.

At the threshold

When attention reaches a certain level of consistency and precision, the system begins to stabilize.

Not because it is controlled, but because it becomes predictable to itself.

The child can anticipate reactions. The signal becomes interpretable.

Adjustments are no longer random. They follow a structure.

This is where learning begins to accumulate.

Not at the moment of correction, but at the moment where correction becomes expected.

What the system is looking for is a signal it can use. Not a correction. A reference.

Beyond the threshold

There is another regime.

When attention becomes too strong, too constant, too precise, the signal changes nature.

It stops being informative.

It becomes dominant.

The system no longer uses the signal to adjust.

It reorganizes itself around the signal.

Behavior becomes aligned with observation rather than with internal state.

The child does not express. The child performs.

Loss of resolution

In this regime, something disappears.

Differences that were previously visible become flattened.

Subtle changes are no longer expressed.

The system loses resolution.

From the outside, behavior appears stable, controlled, consistent.

But this stability is not informative.

It does not reveal the structure of the system. It conceals it.

Intermittent attention

There is a common pattern.

Attention that alternates between absence and intensity.

Long intervals with no observation, followed by sudden correction.

The system does not stabilize in this configuration.

It oscillates between exploration and compression.

No regime lasts long enough to produce structure.

The pattern is recognizable. Not always deliberate. Sometimes it reflects the adult's own rhythm, their own oscillation between presence and absence.

Calibration

Attention cannot be reduced to presence or absence.

It must be calibrated.

Not in a fixed way, but relative to the state of the system.

A signal that stabilizes one configuration may distort another.

The same level of attention does not produce the same effect at different times.

This is why rules are insufficient.

Calibration requires observation of the effect of observation.

What remains

Attention is not neutral.

It is not continuous.

It is not infinite.

It has thresholds.

Below a certain level, it does not structure the system.

Above a certain level, it erases information.

Between these two regimes, something becomes possible.

A signal that can be received without being imposed.

A structure that can form without being forced.

The difficulty is to give a signal that the system can use.
