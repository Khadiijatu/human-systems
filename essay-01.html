What You See Is Not the State

There are children we believe we know.

We see them every day. We hear their voices, we recognize their habits, we think we can anticipate their reactions. If someone were to ask us how they are doing, we would answer without hesitation. We would describe their character, their strengths, their weaknesses.

And yet, if we were asked a more precise question, something slightly more demanding, the confidence would start to weaken.

Not “how is she?”
but “what is the current state of her inner world?”

At that point, most of us would realize that we do not know.

What we have are fragments. A sentence here. A reaction there. A silence that we did not interpret at the time. A moment of joy that we did not examine closely. We hold a handful of points scattered along a trajectory we never truly followed.

What we call knowledge is often a reconstruction built from these few points.

The hidden graph

If one had to represent the inner state of a child, a graph would be more appropriate than a list.

Each node would correspond to something that cannot be directly seen: a belief about oneself, a fear, a desire, a memory, a habit, a wound, a latent ability.

Edges would represent interactions between these nodes. A single remark received at the wrong moment might reinforce a belief, weaken a desire, or activate a dormant fear. A small success, unnoticed by others, might quietly reconfigure several connections at once.

Some nodes would be highly connected. Others would remain isolated, waiting for a condition that might never occur.

The graph evolves. Nodes appear, disappear, reconnect. Entire regions may reorganize without producing any immediately visible effect.

What we observe is what surfaces. Projections.

Observation is not neutral

There is a common belief that attention is a form of affection. Something one gives or withholds.

It is more precise to see it as a measurement process.

A system that evolves without measurement will not stop evolving. It simply evolves without correction.

In classical systems, a sensor allows adjustment. Without it, the system drifts.

In human systems, the situation is more complex. The system reacts to the fact that it is being measured.

An absence of attention often leads to amplification. The system increases the amplitude of its outputs until something is detected. A raised voice, a provocation, an exaggeration.

Excessive attention produces a different effect. The system becomes strategic. It no longer expresses its state, it models the observer and adjusts its outputs accordingly.

In both cases, what is observed is no longer a reliable projection.

Discontinuity

There is a moment that many adults recognize.

A result appears. It may be a success that seems disproportionate, or a failure that feels sudden. A sentence is spoken, a decision is made, a behavior emerges that does not fit the model we had.

The reaction is often the same.

“How did this happen?”

The question is poorly formulated.

It assumes that the event itself is the origin. In most cases, it is only the visible consequence of a process that unfolded without us.

Discontinuity is rarely a property of the system. It is a property of the observation.

A curve is smooth when observed continuously.

Sample it at two distant points, and anything can happen in between.

Imitation and false trajectories

Another phenomenon complicates observation.

A system may reproduce an output without reproducing the underlying process.

A child may imitate confidence without having built it. Repeat words without understanding them. Display behaviors that belong to another trajectory.

From the outside, the projection looks correct.

Internally, the graph is different.

Such configurations are unstable. They hold as long as the environment remains compatible with the borrowed pattern. As soon as conditions change, the structure collapses.

The difficulty is that imitation is efficient in the short term. It produces convincing outputs with minimal effort.

Following a process is slower. It requires passing through intermediate states that are not immediately visible or rewarding.

The preference for imitation is therefore not accidental.

Escape routes

When the process becomes too demanding, the system looks for ways to avoid it.

Distance. Isolation. Excessive sleep. Screens. Material distractions. Repeated displacement between environments.

These are ways to suspend the evolution of the system without confronting it directly.

From the outside, these behaviors may seem unrelated. From a dynamical perspective, they serve a similar function.

They create intervals during which the state evolves without constraint.

What remains

To observe a human system is to accept that most of its structure is hidden, and that what is visible is partial, delayed, and sometimes distorted.

The question is how to work within this limitation.

How to measure without freezing.
How to follow without intruding.
How to detect a change of regime before it becomes irreversible.

This requires precision.

Not more time, necessarily. But better questions. Better sampling. A willingness to admit that what we see is not the state.
